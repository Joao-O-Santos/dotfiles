# Our Approach

We set out to test if discriminatory statements would be perceived as
such when targeting children. Meaning, we aimed to test if the perceived
acceptability of discriminatory statements directed at children differs
from that of an equal statement directed at another minority, for which
there are anti-discrimination norms. Thus, our goal is to study how
acceptable people perceive childism to be, compared to other prejudices.
Obviously, it is hard to speak about the general acceptability of all
childism beliefs, practices, or behaviors. As with other prejudices, the
perceived social acceptability is different for each belief, practice,
or behavior. For instance, in the case of racism, the perceived
acceptability of using racial slurs will be different from that of
telling racist jokes. Moreover, the perceived acceptability of a
specific slur or joke will differ depending on the specifics of the slur
and joke. Notwithstanding, the question of the general, or average,
perceived social acceptability of a prejudice when compared to another
is still worth asking. Let us assume that on average a given prejudice
(e.g., childism) is perceived as more socially acceptable than another
prejudice (e.g., racism). Then, if we were to draw a random sample of
instances of prejudice from both "populations" of instances of
prejudice, we could expect that, on average, participants' would rate
one sample of instances as more acceptable than the other. This seems
like a standard case of statistical hypothesis testing. Thus, the
validity of our inferences about the average acceptability of a
prejudice, should be determined by the same factors that determine the
validity of statistical inferences. Meaning, even though it is not an
easy problem, comparing the perceived acceptability of different
prejudices is a valid empirical question.

The paradigm of our main experiments (i.e., Study 4.1a and 4.1b) is simple.
We first compiled a list of discriminatory statements, drafting a
version directed at children, one directed at other minorities, and, in
the case of Study 4.1a, one directed at pets. Then, we asked participants
to rate all statements, with each participant only seeing a version of
each. To test our hypothesis, we compared the mean ratings for the
statements directed at kids with those of the statements directed at
other minorities. In both studies, for exploratory purposes, we also
asked participants to fill out scales measuring prejudice-related
variables.

In Study 4.1a, participants completed the motivation to control prejudiced
reactions scale (Dunton & Fazio, 1997). High suppressors of prejudice
may display less prejudice directed at target categories for which there
are anti-discrimination, but more prejudice towards categories not
protected by such norms (Crandall et al., 2002). Note, Crandall and
collaborators (2002) did not measure participants suppression of
prejudice with Dunton and Fazio's (1997) scale, but this scale is
arguably measuring a similar construct.

In Study 4.1b, participants filled out the ambivalent sexism scale (Glick
& Fiske, 1996) and the social dominance orientation scale (Pratto et
al., 1994). There are some historical parallels between the treatment of
children and the treatment of women, for instance, both have
historically performed unpaid domestic work (see Nieuwenhuys, 1996).
Moreover, people's attitudes towards children may follow a pattern
similar to benevolent sexism (e.g., children are positively perceived
but may still be discriminated when behaving in a stereotype-incongruent
manner). Thus, including the ambivalent sexism inventory (Glick & Fiske,
1996) scale may reveal some interesting patterns. Historically
discriminated categories have been compared to children in order to
justify the power the majority categories held over them (see Rollo
2018a; 2018b; see also Saminaden et al., 2010). Hence, looking at
participants' social dominance orientation (as measured by Pratto et
al., 1994) may prove insightful.

We also reckoned we should include completely acceptable and completely
unacceptable statements, in both studies, to have participants use the
entire range of the rating scale. To do so we first had to pilot
statements that would be perceived as either one. After the pilot, we
conducted Study 4.1a in Portugal, with a Portuguese-speaking sample,
which supported our hypothesis. Later, we replicated our results, in
Amazon's Mechanical Turk, with an English-speaking sample. After
presenting and discussing the results from both studies, we review the
possible societal implications, and suggest possible follow-up studies.
