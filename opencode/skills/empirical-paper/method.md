## Method


### Participants

Thirty-one undergrads (25, 80.06% female; *M*~age~ = 19.10, SD =
2.61) consented to participate. Thirty were psychology students, and one
reported studying other sciences. All participants were Portuguese
native speakers. Two (6.45%) participants were Brazilian, and all others
were Portuguese nationals. Regarding participants' frequency of contact
with children (multiple selections allowed), one (3.23%) reported it as
very rare, five (16.13%) as rare, 13 (41.94%) as occasional, nine
(29.03%) as frequent, and three (9.68%) as very frequent. Regarding
participants' relationship with children, 11 (35.48%) reported having
siblings, 20 (64.52%) cousins, three (9.68%) nephews/nieces, two (6.45%)
had students of their own, four (12.90%) otherwise worked with children,
nine (29.03%) had children as their neighbors, and nine (29.03%)
selected other. The open-ended responses from the nine (29.03%)
participants who selected other, tell us that two (6.45%) reported
having children as their friends; one (3.23%) interacting with children
in a kindergarten; one (3.23%) with children at the scouts (boys and
girls allowed in Portuguese scout clubs, other than the girl scouts
"guias"); one (3.23%) reported interacting with children that were
relatives of friends; one (3.23%) with children of their parents'
friends; one (3.23%) interacting with children at dance school; one
(3.23%) with their friends' siblings; and one (3.23%) interacting with
children in a volunteer project.


### Materials

Twenty-four statements, 12 expected to be perceived as completely
acceptable, and 12 expected to be rated as completely unacceptable, made
up the materials for this study. For brevity's sake the complete
statement list is not presented here but is available in the
supplementary materials.


### Procedure

Participants came to the lab and consented to participate. The
instructions explained participants were going to read some statements,
and that their task was to drag a slider over a bar with a label on each
end. The left-end of the bar was labeled "completely unacceptable", and
the right-end "completely acceptable". The instructions also reminded
participants that if they wanted to keep the bar on its initial location
(i.e., the center) they had to click on it for their response to be
recorded. We programmed this pilot in Qualtrics, and set it to record
the slider's position on a 100-point scale (but participants were
unaware of how many points the scale had). After rating all statements,
in a random order, participants provided their demographics, namely,
their age, gender, occupation, native language, nationality, the
frequency of contact with children, and the nature of their relationship
with the children they come into contact with (e.g., siblings, parents,
neighbors).
