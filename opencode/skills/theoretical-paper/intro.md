# Sorting Out the Replication Crisis with Philosophy of Science

*"Nothing in life is to be feared, it is only to be understood. Now is
the time to understand more, so that we may fear less."* (Marie Curie)

Reminiscent of an economic crisis, the so-called replication crisis in
science (RSC) created a climate of uncertainty, provoking emotional
reactions and radical calls to action. After economic crashes some
experts may crunch the numbers looking to improve economic models, while
others may call for new regulations on the markets to deter investor
misconduct. In the case of the RSC, some critics argued for improving
statistical models (Amrhein & Greenland, 2018; Amrhein et al., 2019;
Gelman & Loken, 2013; Stark & Saltelli, 2018). Other critics made the
case for restructuring incentive structures to stop pressuring
researchers to engage in questionable research practices (QRPs, see
Simmons et al., 2011; John et al., 2012). There were also those who
developed tools to detect evidence of publication-bias, *p*-hacking or
fraud (e.g., Schimmack, 2014; Simonsohn et al., 2014; Schimmack &
Brunner, 2017).

Public debates on economic crises may neglect insights from economic
theory and related fields on how we can improve our economies. Likewise,
we believe the RSC debate largely ignored insights from the philosophy
of science. Even though some authors have discussed such insights (see
Earp & Tafimow, 2015; Eronen & Bringmann, 2021; Fiedler, 2017; Flis,
2019; Holtz & Monnerjahn 2017; Holtz, 2020; Morawski, 2019; Wiggins &
Chrisopherson, 2019), highly relevant philosophical perspectives,
particularly the New Experimentalism (Hacking, 1983; Mayo, 1996), have
mostly been left out of this discussion. Aiming to fill this gap, we
draw from foundational theories of the philosophy of science to present
recommendations for how to overcome the RSC.

We start by reviewing how the RSC unfolded and how perverse incentive
structures inadvertently lead researchers to trade quality for quantity
creating a myriad of scientific pitfalls. We suggest that different
perspectives on the root causes of the RCPS led critics to make widely
different proposals to overcome it. Then, we draw from foundational
theories of the philosophy of science to reevaluate the RSC, namely, the
identified pitfalls and the proposed interventions to address them.
Finally, we continue to draw from philosophy of science to suggest old
but untried solutions for moving forward our field.
