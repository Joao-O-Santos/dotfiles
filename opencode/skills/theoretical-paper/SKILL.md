# Skill: theoretical-paper

Design, outline, and write theoretical papers from rough notes, following
STYLE.md and structure.md.

Read the root `../../STYLE.md` for shared conventions (voice, tone, 72-char
wrap, hamburger paragraphs, etc.).

## Default workflow

1. **Clarify** – paraphrase the thesis in 3-5 sentences; identify
   critique, proposal, audience.
2. **Outline** – propose 1-3 outline options with section headings and
   rhetorical purpose per section. Present to the user before drafting.
3. **Draft** – one section at a time; topic-sentence-first paragraphs;
   `<!-- TODO: cite -->` for missing references.
4. **Revise** – tighten claims, verify central thesis is explicit,
   confirm critiques are fair.

## Revision workflow

When revising an existing theoretical draft:

1. **Reverse outline** – extract the topic sentence from each paragraph;
   present the resulting outline to the user for structural feedback
   before any prose changes.
2. **Structural revision** – reorder, merge, split, or cut sections
   based on the reverse outline.
3. **Claim tightening** – ensure each major claim is explicit and
   supported; flag claims that are vague, overbroad, or under-argued.
4. **Prose polish** – sentence-level clarity, concision, and tone
   alignment with STYLE.md.
5. **Final check** – verify that critiques of opposing views are fair,
   strawman-free, and proportional; verify that claim scope matches
   user intent.

## Style conventions

- Confident, measured, analytic tone (see STYLE.md §3).
- Separate: descriptive background / normative diagnosis / positive
  proposal / implications.
- Use an introduction and literature review similar to empirical papers,
  but rely more on **examples, metaphors, and thought experiments**
  that reappear throughout the paper to keep abstractions grounded.
- Organize the body into **arguments, not authors**: each major section
  should advance one key claim, supported by sub-claims, evidence, and
  engagement with counter-arguments.
- Conclude by synthesizing how the proposed perspective changes what
  researchers should do, measure, or interpret differently in future
  empirical and theoretical work.

### Norm-challenging conventions

When going against the system/current norms:
- Clearly demonstrate **understanding of the current norm**, then explain
  why a shift is warranted and highlight continuity where possible to
  reduce perceived upheaval.
- Show the change is not that big and the payoff is worth the effort.
- Keep critiques of existing norms fair and accurate.
- Match claim scope to user intent; do not quietly universalize narrow
  arguments.

## Guardrails

- Do not invent citations; use `<!-- TODO: cite X -->` placeholders.
- Keep within word-count constraints; suggest cuts when needed.
- Mark uncertain claims explicitly.
