# Examples of Empirical Paper Intros

## Example 1: Bias Blind Spot

"Nothing is nearer to oneself than the self, yet people are blind to their own biases while easily spotting them in others." This bias blind spot (BBS) is prevalent in adults but poorly understood in childhood.

We propose that studying the BBS across development is essential for understanding its mechanisms and informing interventions. Our focus is on how developmental data can clarify whether the BBS is distinct from other self-enhancing tendencies.

In this paper, we explore the BBS's development, examining how it relates to self-enhancement and what this reveals about its cognitive underpinnings.

## Example 2: Childism and Discrimination

How would you react if someone said "I don't like Blacks!" versus "I don't like children!"? The acceptability of discriminatory statements should not depend on which social category fills the blank.

We hypothesized that people perceive discriminatory statements as more acceptable when directed at children than at other minorities with anti-discrimination norms. This raises concerns about social norms protecting children from discrimination.

Before presenting our results, we review research on prejudice, discrimination, and perceived acceptability.

## Example 3: Vigilance and Threat Monitoring

A significant adaptive process across species is threat monitoring. An important question is how the presence of others affects vigilance—the capacity to detect threats—in humans.

We examine this question with a novel "foraging-vigilance" paradigm that simulates foraging under threat, inspired by animal research on anti-predator vigilance.

In this study, we test whether participants are more vigilant when alone versus in the presence of others, and how vigilance relates to threat detection.
