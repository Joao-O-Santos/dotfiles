We set out to test if discriminatory statements would be perceived as such when targeting children. Meaning, we aimed to test if the perceived acceptability of discriminatory statements directed at children differs from that of an equal statement directed at another minority, for which there are anti-discrimination norms. Thus, our goal is to study how acceptable people perceive childism to be, compared to other prejudices.

Obviously, it is hard to speak about the general acceptability of all childism beliefs, practices, or behaviors. As with other prejudices, the perceived social acceptability is different for each belief, practice, or behavior. For instance, in the case of racism, the perceived acceptability of using racial slurs will be different from that of telling racist jokes. Moreover, the perceived acceptability of a specific slur or joke will differ depending on the specifics of the slur and joke.

Notwithstanding, the question of the general, or average, perceived social acceptability of a prejudice when compared to another is still worth asking. Let us assume that on average a given prejudice (e.g., childism) is perceived as more socially acceptable than another prejudice (e.g., racism). Then, if we were to draw a random sample of instances of prejudice from both "populations" of instances of prejudice, we could expect that, on average, participants' would rate one sample of instances as more acceptable than the other.

This seems like a standard case of statistical hypothesis testing. Thus, the validity of our inferences about the average acceptability of a prejudice, should be determined by the same factors that determine the validity of statistical inferences. Meaning, even though it is not an easy problem, comparing the perceived acceptability of different prejudices is a valid empirical question.
